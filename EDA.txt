Fig. 4.1.2a shows that a significant proportion of days in Delhi fall into the

From Fig 4.1.2b: Positive skewness (where the mean is greater than the median)

The histogram Fig 4.1.2d and the kernel density estimation (KDE) plot represent
a two-peaked shape of distribution. The first peak can be observed around
AQI ≈ 100 for the summer/monsoon period, while the second one lies at
AQI ≈ 320 for the winter period.

Box plot by year Fig 4.1.2e reveals the fact that pollution peaks (upper
whisker) stay high for the whole decade and exceed the value of 400–500 in
winter. There is also an increasing trend in the median AQI from 2015–2018 to
2019–2024.

From Fig 4.1.2f: The PM10 value is 219.4 µg/m³, while PM2.5 is 149.8 µg/m³,
both of which are much higher than the concentrations of any other pollutants.
The highest value among the gases is NO2 at 46.5 µg/m³, followed by O3 and SO2
at 20.0 µg/m³ and 12.8 µg/m³, respectively. CO stands at 2.86 mg/m³ on a
different unit scale. This profile confirms that Delhi's air quality problem is
predominantly a particulate matter crisis.

The violin plot Fig 4.1.2g shows that an increase in PM2.5 is nearly a straight
path to deterioration in the AQI class. Regarding the Severe class, the median
PM2.5 exceeds 300 µg/m³, which is more than 20 times the WHO guideline of
15 µg/m³ over 24 hours. The shape width for each violin depicts the variability
in daily values — the wider the violin, the more variable the data.

Fig 4.2.1a  Correlation Matrix (AQI vs. Individual Pollutants)
---------------------------------------------------------------
Full Correlation Matrix (All Features)

Several clear trends can be identified from the correlation matrix Fig 4.2.1a.
From the last row (AQI correlations):

  PM2.5       r =  0.98   (primary AQI driver)
  PM10        r =  0.97   (primary AQI driver)
  CO          r =  0.95   (combustion indicator)
  NOx         r =  0.95   (vehicular emissions)
  NO          r =  0.93   (combustion by-product)
  Temperature r = -0.73   (negatively correlated)
  Wind Speed  r = -0.72   (negatively correlated)
  Humidity    r = -0.07   (minimal effect)

Among pollutants, there are highly correlated factors (r > 0.90), including
PM2.5, PM10, CO, NO, and NOx.

To quantify the pairwise relationship between AQI and each individual
pollutant, scatter plots with fitted OLS regression lines were made for
AQI vs. PM2.5 and AQI vs. PM10.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Fig 4.2.1b  Scatter Plots: PM2.5 vs AQI, PM10 vs AQI, and Wind Speed vs AQI
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------

  PM2.5 vs AQI   r = 0.9763
  PM10  vs AQI   r = 0.9744

The scatter plots Fig 4.2.1b give visual evidence for the correlation findings.
PM2.5 and PM10 have nearly linear correlations with AQI; their data form
straight lines. The correlation between Wind Speed and AQI is negative and
nonlinear, but only when Wind Speed is greater than 12 km/h; AQI values are
never more than 200.

4.3 TEMPORAL TRENDS & SEASONAL PATTERNS
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Fig 4.3.1a  Seasonal Trends in Daily AQI (2015–2024) with CPCB Classification
            and 30-Day Rolling Mean
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Fig 4.3.1a illustrates the annual time series of AQI values over 10 years,
colored according to the NAQI classification. Each year, an annual cycle is
observable; in particular, the values become Satisfactory/Good during the
monsoon period and peak at red/purple (Very Poor/Severe) from November to
January. This trend is consistently observable throughout all ten years. In
addition, the black line denotes a 30-day rolling mean, which eliminates
short-run fluctuations and captures the underlying cycle. Finally, the lockdown
due to the COVID-19 pandemic period (Apr–Jun 2020) dips below the average level.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Fig 4.3.1b  Rolling Moving Averages of AQI (7, 30, 90 Days) with CPCB
            Threshold Levels
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

The rolling MA chart Fig 4.3.1b overlays three smoothing windows against the
CPCB threshold lines:

  7-day  MA (blue)  : Tracks short-term spikes, including individual pollution
                      events and rapid monsoon-driven cleanups.
  30-day MA (orange): Captures the month-scale seasonal wave.
  90-day MA (red)   : Traces the long-run seasonal envelope, peaking at
                      approximately 350–380 in December–January and bottoming
                      at 80–100 in August–September.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Fig 4.3.1c  Monthly AQI Profile for Each Year (2015–2024) with Grand Mean Overlay
----------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The monthly profile Fig 4.3.1c demonstrates a consistent U-shaped seasonal
pattern across all 10 years:

  - AQI peaks in November–January (winter)
  - Drops sharply through the monsoon season (June–September)
  - Recovers rapidly from October onward
  - November records the worst average AQI of 407.7 (Severe), attributable to
    the convergence of post-harvest stubble burning, temperature inversions, and
    reduced wind dispersion.
  - The 2020 profile (pink line) stands out with an anomalously low AQI in
    April–June, directly attributable to COVID-19 lockdown emission reductions.
