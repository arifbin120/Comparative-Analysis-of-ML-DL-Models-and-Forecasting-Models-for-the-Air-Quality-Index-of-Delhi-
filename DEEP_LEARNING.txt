TABLE 6. ANN CLASSIFICATION MODEL ARCHITECTURES
================================================================================

Three Artificial Neural Network (ANN) architectures were developed for a
six-class classification problem using 12 input features.

  Model              Architecture                        Activation  Alpha(α)  Epochs
  -----------------  ----------------------------------  ----------  --------  ------
  ANN — Shallow      Input(12)→64→32→Output(6)           ReLU        0.001     60
  ANN — Deep         Input(12)→256→128→64→32→Output(6)   ReLU        0.0005    65
  ANN — Regularized  Input(12)→128→64→32→Output(6)       Tanh        0.01      68

Architecture Descriptions:

  ANN — Shallow:
    Consists of two hidden layers (64 and 32 neurons) with ReLU activation,
    a learning rate of 0.001, and 60 training epochs. Fewest parameters;
    suitable for lightweight/fast deployment.

  ANN — Deep:
    Includes four hidden layers (256, 128, 64, and 32 neurons) with ReLU
    activation, a lower learning rate of 0.0005, and 65 epochs, allowing it
    to capture more complex patterns. Best overall performance.

  ANN — Regularized:
    Uses three hidden layers (128, 64, and 32 neurons) with Tanh activation,
    a learning rate of 0.01, and 68 epochs, aiming to improve generalization
    and reduce overfitting. Best for detecting minority classes (Good/Severe).

These architectures were compared to determine the most effective model for
classification performance.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ANN — Deep (Best Performing Model)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The deep neural network architecture performed best across all three
architectures for all metrics. Its test accuracy was 89.23%, and its weighted
F1-score was 0.8924, indicating it performed excellently and consistently across
all six CPCB AQI classes. Notably, its macro F1-score was significantly higher
compared to those of the other two architectures. The macro F1-score gives equal
weight to all classes, regardless of their representation in the dataset. In this
regard, the high value indicates that the architecture performs well on minority
classes, such as Good or Severe, which are less common in urban air quality
datasets.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ANN — Regularized
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
This model, although the most regularized, achieved fairly competitive results,
with an accuracy of 87.41% and an F1 score of 0.8740. The macro F1 score is
significantly higher at 0.8441 than that of the shallow model.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ANN — Shallow (Baseline)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The shallow model achieved a test accuracy of 86.86% and a weighted F1 score of
0.8664, which are reasonable for a network with two hidden layers. Nonetheless,
a macro F1 score of 0.7972 or lower suggests challenges in accurately
categorizing minority AQI levels.

================================================================================
PRECISION & RECALL PER CLASS (ANN Deep)
================================================================================

What it shows: The two components of F1 — Precision and Recall — displayed
separately for the best model (ANN Deep) across all six categories. This reveals
the nature of errors, not just their quantity.

  Precision: Of all days the model predicted as Class X, what fraction were
             actually Class X? (Avoids false alarms)

  Recall:    Of all days that were actually Class X, what fraction did the model
             find? (Avoids missing real cases)

Public Health Significance of the Severe error: The classifier accurately
predicts Severe conditions 93% of the time, but misses 17% of actual Severe
days. In these cases, the classifier labels those days as Very Poor. This is the
conservative approach because issuing a Very Poor warning on a Severe day is
less risky than issuing a Good warning on a Severe day.
